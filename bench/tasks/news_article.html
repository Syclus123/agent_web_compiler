Record Heatwave Prompts Emergency Water Restrictions Across Southwest

Temperatures exceeding 115°F for the fifth consecutive day force unprecedented conservation measures affecting 12 million residents

By Maria Chen, Climate Correspondent July 18, 2025, 2:30 PM ET 6 min read
Share on Twitter Share on Facebook Email

PHOENIX — State and local officials across the American Southwest announced emergency water-use restrictions on Thursday as an unprecedented heatwave pushed temperatures above 115°F (46°C) for the fifth consecutive day, straining power grids and depleting already-diminished reservoir levels.

The restrictions, which take effect immediately, prohibit outdoor watering between 6 AM and 8 PM, ban the filling of swimming pools, and require commercial car washes to suspend operations. Violations carry fines of up to $500 for first offenses and $2,000 for repeat violations.

Scope of the Crisis

The emergency measures affect approximately 12 million residents across Arizona, Nevada, and parts of Southern California. Phoenix recorded a high of 118°F on Wednesday, breaking the previous record of 116°F set in June 2024. Las Vegas reached 117°F, while Palm Springs hit 122°F.

"This is not a normal summer hot spell," said Dr. Elena Vasquez, director of the National Weather Service's Southwest Region. "We are seeing temperatures that our infrastructure and water systems were never designed to handle on a sustained basis."

Water Supply Under Pressure

Lake Mead, the largest reservoir in the United States and a critical water source for 25 million people, has dropped to just 22% capacity. Lake Powell stands at 28% capacity. Both levels represent historic lows, surpassing the previous records set during the 2021-2022 drought.

The Central Arizona Project, which delivers Colorado River water to Phoenix and Tucson, announced a 30% reduction in deliveries to agricultural users effective August 1. Municipal supplies remain unaffected for now, but officials warned that continued extreme heat could trigger further cuts.

Health and Human Impact

Maricopa County reported 847 heat-related emergency room visits in the past week, a 340% increase over the same period last year. Fourteen deaths have been attributed to hyperthermia, including three outdoor workers and two elderly residents without air conditioning.

The Federal Emergency Management Agency has deployed mobile cooling centers across the region, and utility companies have implemented a moratorium on power shutoffs through the end of the month.

Long-Term Outlook

Climate scientists say the heatwave, while extreme, fits a well-documented pattern of increasing summer temperatures in the Southwest. Average July temperatures in Phoenix have risen 4.2°F since 1970, and the number of days exceeding 110°F has nearly doubled in the past two decades.

"We need to stop treating these events as anomalies," said Professor James Whitfield of Arizona State University's School of Sustainability. "This is the new baseline, and our water management, urban planning, and public health systems must adapt accordingly."
